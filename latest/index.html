Cover Story · Live · June 7, 2026 · Kia Forum · Los Angeles

Rush Returns. Anika Nilles Kills It. The World Exhales.

Sources Rolling Stone ↗ Loudwire ↗ Modern Drummer ↗ Ultimate Classic Rock ↗

On June 7, Rush played their first concert in eleven years at the Kia Forum in Los Angeles — the same venue where they ended the R40 tour in 2015. Geddy Lee and Alex Lifeson took the stage. Neil Peart did not. In his place: Anika Nilles — a German drummer whose YouTube performances had circulated among serious drummers for years. She played two sets, drew from a pool of 40+ Rush classics, and delivered 2 hours 40 minutes of drumming that Rolling Stone called "magical."

She had months to learn one of the most technically demanding catalogs in rock. She described her prep to Rick Beato directly: "Sometimes I was sitting there and thought, 'I have no idea how I'm learning that.' But somehow it's working."

Mike Portnoy watched the full RushCon livestream from home and called the setlist "absolutely PERFECT." The tour has now expanded to 88 shows across 24 cities, running through North America in 2026 and into South America and Europe in 2027. Over half a million tickets sold. The conversation around who belongs behind a serious rock kit shifted that night — not with a statement, but with a performance that left no room for debate.

Issue 01 · June 2026 Lesson
Ghost Notes: The Quiet Power Behind the Groove
Intermediate

Ghost notes are soft, barely audible snare hits played between your primary accents. They don't announce themselves — they fill space, add texture, and make a groove feel alive instead of mechanical. Think of them as the difference between a drum machine and a human.

You'll hear them everywhere: David Garibaldi with Tower of Power, Steve Jordan, Questlove. Once you hear them, you can't unhear them. Once you practice them, you can't leave them out.

The goal this month: get your ghost notes consistent, quiet, and controlled — right hand free to accent, left hand filling with whispers. Start at 60 BPM. Tempo doesn't matter yet. Evenness does.

01
Set Your Baseline Dynamic
Play a normal backbeat (beats 2 & 4) at comfortable volume. That's your accent level. Now play the snare at ~20% of that — barely touching the head. That's a ghost note. Train your ear to hear the gap before you fill it.
02
The Basic Pattern
Over a simple kick/snare groove, add a ghost note on the "e" of beat 1 and the "ah" of beat 3 at 60 BPM. The ghost note should disappear into the groove, not compete with the backbeat for attention.
03
Isolation Practice
Play only ghost notes for one minute — no accents, just a stream of quiet, even hits. Stroke height: 1–2 inches off the head. Wrist does the work; arm stays quiet. This builds the muscle memory for consistency.
04
Bring It Into a Song
Pick a song you know and add ghost notes between your backbeats. Start with one per bar. Notice how the groove breathes differently. Listen back — you'll hear the difference immediately.
60–80 BPM · 15 min daily · Use a metronome

Culture · Streaming
The Livestream Is the New Club Gig
For two decades, the path for a working drummer was: learn, rehearse, land a band, play bars, climb. That ladder still exists — but a parallel track has emerged. Live drum streaming offers something the bar circuit never could: a global room, on your schedule, with real-time feedback and a community that returns every week. The gear has caught up (Roland TD-50, hybrid modules, NDI setups), the platforms are stable, and the audience is growing. The question now isn't whether streaming is legitimate. It's whether you're building it with intention.
02
Culture · Representation
What Anika Nilles Did for Women Behind the Kit
There was no campaign around it. No think-pieces previewing the moment. Anika Nilles just showed up, played Tom Sawyer in front of 17,000 people, and left the conversation permanently changed. The drumming world has always had exceptional women — Sheila E., Terri Lyne Carrington, Cindy Blackman Santana — but mainstream rock rarely makes room. Nilles stepping into the most scrutinized drum seat of the decade and owning it is a reference point that will be cited for years. Not as a diversity story. As a drumming story.
03
Culture · Production History
The Breakbeat Never Left. It Just Leveled Up.
Toontrack's Drum & Bass EZX isn't just a library drop — it's a reminder that the breakbeat is the foundation of electronic music's DNA. From the Amen Break powering jungle in 1992 to Andy Gangadeen tracking through a Decca valve console in 2026, the throughline is unbroken. Electronic production elevated the drummer's role — it just changed where the kit sits in the chain.
04
Culture · Technology
Your Practice Room Is Now a Studio
The JBL BandBox Trio and Yamaha EAD50 represent a larger shift. The gap between practicing and producing is collapsing. AI-powered audio separation, hybrid trigger/acoustic modules, and accessible interfaces mean the bedroom drummer in 2026 can achieve reference-quality sound that would have required a professional studio five years ago.
